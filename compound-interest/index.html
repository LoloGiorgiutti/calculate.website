What is compound interest?
Interest earned is added to the principal and also earns interest in the next period. The compounding frequency defines how often this effect is applied — the more frequent, the higher the final return. Over time, the gap between what you invest and the final value grows exponentially.
Frequently asked questions
What is compound interest?
Compound interest is when the interest you earn is added to your principal, and that larger amount also earns interest. Unlike simple interest, the balance grows exponentially over time.
Why is compound interest called the eighth wonder of the world?
The phrase illustrates the power of exponential growth. With moderate rates and long time horizons, capital can multiply many times. At 10% annual rate, $10,000 grows to $67,275 in 20 years.
What is the Rule of 72?
The Rule of 72 is a quick shortcut: divide 72 by the annual interest rate to estimate how long it takes to double your money. At 8% per year, that's approximately 9 years (72 ÷ 8 = 9).
What is the difference between APR and APY?
APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the nominal rate without compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) includes the effect of compounding and reflects the true annual return — always ≥ APR.
