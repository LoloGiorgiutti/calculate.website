Frequently asked questions
What is the Least Common Multiple (LCM)?
The LCM of two or more integers is the smallest positive integer that is divisible by all of them. For example, LCM(4, 6) = 12 because 12 is the smallest number divisible by both 4 and 6.
How is the LCM calculated?
The most efficient method uses the relationship LCM(a,b) = |a × b| / GCD(a,b). The GCD is found using the Euclidean algorithm. For multiple numbers the LCM is computed iteratively: LCM(a,b,c) = LCM(LCM(a,b), c).
What is the difference between LCM and GCD?
The GCD (Greatest Common Divisor) is the largest number that divides all inputs exactly. The LCM is the smallest number that all inputs divide into exactly. They are related by: LCM(a,b) × GCD(a,b) = a × b.
What is the LCM used for?
LCM is used when adding or subtracting fractions (finding a common denominator), scheduling recurring events that happen on different cycles, and solving problems in number theory and modular arithmetic.
Can the LCM be calculated for more than two numbers?
Yes. Compute the LCM of the first two, then compute the LCM of that result with the third number, and so on. This calculator supports up to 10 numbers at once.
