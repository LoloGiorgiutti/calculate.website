Frequently asked questions
How much protein do I need per day?
It depends on your goal. For general health: 0.6–0.8 g per pound of body weight (1.2–1.6 g/kg). For muscle gain: 0.8–1.0 g per pound (1.8–2.2 g/kg). For fat loss while preserving muscle: 0.7–0.9 g per pound (1.6–2.0 g/kg). This calculator applies ranges based on the goal you select.
Which foods are highest in protein?
Top sources: chicken breast cooked (~31g/100g), tuna in water (~26g/100g), lean beef (~26g/100g), Greek yogurt (~10g/100g), cottage cheese (~12g/100g), eggs (~6g each), and cooked lentils (~9g/100g).
Do I need protein supplements?
No. A varied diet can meet your protein needs without supplements. Protein powders (whey, casein, plant-based) are a convenient option when it's hard to hit your target through food alone, but they're not superior to whole-food sources.
What happens if I eat too much protein?
For healthy people without kidney disease, high protein intake is generally safe. Excess protein is converted to energy or stored as fat. A sudden large increase may cause digestive discomfort. If you have any medical condition, consult your doctor.
How much protein is in one egg?
One large egg contains about 6–7g of protein. The white provides most (~4g) with almost no fat, while the yolk contributes 2–3g along with healthy fats, vitamin D, and other micronutrients.
